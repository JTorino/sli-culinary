The program

How the program works.

Who this is for

A graduate of a Rwandan technical culinary school who wants to launch or manage a restaurant within the next twenty-four months.

You probably:

  • Have one to five years of professional kitchen experience.
  • Cook to a professional standard already.
  • Have a business idea, even if rough.
  • Want to own the business eventually, not just work in it.
  • Can commit to five days a week for six months.
Cohort structure

Eight students per intake. Four per session.

Each intake admits eight students, split into two cohorts of four. The morning cohort runs 8 AM – 1 PM; the afternoon cohort runs 1 PM – 6 PM. Same content, same facilitators, same labs.

Cohort size is small on purpose: four students per session means real attention and real relationships.

Weekly rhythm

Same five-day pattern every week.

Content changes; rhythm does not.

  • Monday — Digital Fluency Lab

    Computer, AI, and spreadsheet skills used in a real restaurant.

  • Tuesday — Session 1

    Lecture and discussion on the week's main topic.

  • Wednesday — Session 2

    Second lecture, deeper into the same topic.

  • Thursday — Service Lab

    Book club and hospitality practice.

  • Friday — Main Lab

    Practical kitchen work, prep service, or capstone build.

Throughout the program

Plus the rituals that hold a six-month program together.

  • Quizzes every two weeks.
  • Capstone milestones at the end of each month.
  • Mentorship — one-on-one with assigned industry mentor, monthly.
  • Recurring journals — Daily Meeze, Service Journal.
What you do, not just learn

By the end you will have:

  • Registered (or fully drafted) a real business with RDB.
  • Built a unit-economics model in Google Sheets.
  • Costed ten menu items at real Kigali supplier prices.
  • Negotiated face-to-face with at least one real supplier.
  • Designed a brand identity, a trilingual menu, and thirty days of social media content.
  • Run prep service for two real outside events.
  • Completed Date Code Genie food-safety certification.
  • Drafted a HACCP plan for your capstone.
  • Pitched your business to a panel of industry experts.
  • Hosted a real soft-launch event with twenty-five guests on the final day.
Cost & grants

Heavily subsidised through donor support.

STRIVELABS INITIATIVE is a non-profit. Tuition for accepted students is heavily subsidised; the application asks about your financial situation so we can match you to the right level of aid.

Every student who completes receives a $1,000 capstone seed grant. Top capstone presentations are eligible for additional support and introductions to BDF, Inkomoko, and Hanga Pitchfest.

The curriculum

Six months,
month by month.

Each month builds on the one before. The first three are about the business — foundations, costing, brand. The next three are about the business in motion — operations, hospitality, launch.

— Month 01

Foundations

Look around. Before we plan anything, we look at what already exists.

Topics: restaurant types and business models, profit structures and revenue streams, the delivery economy and ghost kitchens.

Restaurant types covered: fast food, casual dining, fine dining, street food, catering, delivery-only kitchens.

By end of month: business name registered or filed at RDB, capstone track chosen, first one-page concept pitch submitted.

— Month 02

Menu Costing & Engineering

The single most important number in food. Get it right or be quietly bankrupt.

Topics: food cost percentage, markup pricing, break-even analysis, portion control, supplier cost management. The Kasavana–Smith menu engineering matrix becomes a tool you will use for years.

Woven in: nutrition and allergen literacy across Weeks 7 and 8 — designing menus for guests with peanut, tree-nut, dairy, egg, soy, wheat, fish, and shellfish allergies, and balanced plates for children, athletes, and elderly customers.

By end of month: ten menu items costed at real prices, twelve-month sourcing calendar built, real supplier negotiation completed.

— Month 03

Menu Design & Branding

Your menu is your top salesperson. Pay them well. Train them.

Topics: pricing psychology, visual hierarchy, customer decision behavior, brand positioning, trilingual menu design (Kinyarwanda, English, French), digital presence on Instagram, TikTok, WhatsApp Business, Google Business Profile.

By end of month: locked brand identity (name, logo, colors, typography), trilingual menu in Canva, thirty-day social content calendar with the first week shot and posted.

— Month 04

Kitchen Management

Set up before you start. Then start clean. Then finish what you started.

Built around Dan Charnas's Work Clean.

Topics: mise-en-place systems, prep lists, inventory control, waste reduction, food safety and HACCP, brigade systems for small Rwandan kitchens. Date Code Genie certification this month.

By end of month: full HACCP plan for your capstone, two real prep services completed, kitchen role rotation across prep, line, and expedite.

— Month 05

Leadership & Hospitality

Service is what you do. Hospitality is how you make people feel.

Built around Will Guidara's Unreasonable Hospitality and Danny Meyer's Setting the Table.

Topics: hospitality mindset, guest experience design, emotional intelligence, service culture, hiring for the right traits, leading a Rwandan team within Rwandan labour and privacy law.

By end of month: complete hospitality and service system plan, ten service standards, daily lineup template, customer journey map, real mock service with twelve live guests.

— Month 06

Launch & Capstone

The work of six months becomes a real launch.

Topics: capital and financing in Rwanda (BDF, Inkomoko, Hanga Pitchfest), pre-opening compliance, RSSB and PAYE, sustainability and packaging under Rwanda's plastic ban (Law N° 17/2019), full food labeling specification.

By end of month: locked launch schedule, twelve-month cash flow forecast, ten-slide pitch deck, capstone presentation to a panel of judges, real soft launch event with twenty-five guests on the final Friday.

Required reading

Four books carry the program.

  • Setting the Table — Danny Meyer (Months 1–4)
  • Unreasonable Hospitality — Will Guidara (Month 5)
  • Work Clean — Dan Charnas (Month 4)
  • Restaurant Success by the Numbers — Roger Fields (Months 1 and 6)

The reading shelf

Books, talks, and tools we use.

Anything we use in class is here, with links to the source. Reading two or three of these will tell you whether the program is right for you.

— 01
Setting the Table
Danny Meyer

The book on hospitality. Meyer's 51 percent rule (hire for emotional skills, train for technical) shapes how we hire and lead.

Free borrow on Internet Archive →
— 02
Unreasonable Hospitality
Will Guidara

The 95/5 rule: tight discipline 95 percent of the time so you can spend foolishly on hospitality the other 5 percent. We read this across Month 5.

Publisher site →
— 03
Work Clean
Dan Charnas

The ten ingredients of mise-en-place applied to running a business. The Daily Meeze practice we follow throughout the program comes from this book.

Author site →

Restaurant Success by the Numbers
Roger Fields

The only one of the five written by an accountant who became a restaurateur. The financial spine of Months 1 and 6.

Tools we teach

Every tool here is free or has a free tier.

  • Google Workspace — for everything written, calculated, scheduled, or shared.
  • Canva — for menus, logos, social content. Free for nonprofits and education.

  • Claude (Anthropic) — for AI-assisted research, drafting, translation; with always-on human verification.

Site visits

Site visits to
industry experts.

Site visits expose students to real industry experts in their place of work — restaurants, hotels, packaging suppliers, cooperatives, RSB-certified producers.

For students

Five working days ahead.

Complete the Site Visit Request form at least five working days before the visit. The form covers permission, funding, and (if you are under eighteen) parental consent and chaperone assignment.

The coordinator responds within three working days.

Open the Site Visit Request form →
For industry hosts

If a student asks to visit you, here is what to expect.

  • The student will contact you ahead with a short email — what they want to learn and three specific questions.
  • Most visits are sixty to ninety minutes: about forty-five minutes of observation, fifteen to thirty minutes of questions.
  • Students bring a notebook and ask permission before photographs.
  • If the student is under eighteen, an SLI chaperone accompanies them for the entire visit.
  • Students write a one-page reflection shared with their cohort, not a public review of your business.

If you cannot host that day, please reply (even briefly). Students learn as much from a kind decline as from a yes.

Apply

Apply for the
next cohort.

The application takes about thirty minutes.

After you submit, you will hear from us within two weeks. Shortlisted applicants are invited to a thirty-minute conversation with a facilitator.

Eligibility

To apply, you should be:

  • A graduate of a recognised technical culinary school in Rwanda, or have equivalent professional kitchen experience.
  • Available Mon–Fri for six months (AM cohort 8 AM – 1 PM, or PM cohort 1 PM – 6 PM).
  • Equipped with a smartphone and a computer (laptop ideally — the program uses spreadsheets and design tools).
  • Able to read and write in English; strong Kinyarwanda; ideally some French.
  • Sixteen or older. Applicants under eighteen include parental consent in their application.
What we ask

One Google Form, five sections.

  • 01 — About you

    Basic contact and background.

  • 02 — Culinary training and work experience

    Where you trained, where you have worked.

  • 03 — Your business idea

    Even rough is fine.

  • 04 — Why this program, in your own words

    The most important section. Be specific.

  • 05 — Financial situation and any aid you would need

    Honest answers help us match you to the right level of subsidy.

Ready

Open the application.

The form lives in Google Forms. Allow about thirty minutes of focused time.
